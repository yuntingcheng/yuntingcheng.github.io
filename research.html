Studying Intra-Halo Light in near-IR Extragalactic Background Light

Cheng, Y.-T. + CIBER collaboration 2020 in prep.

Extragalactic Background Light (EBL) is the integrated emission from all extragalactic sources along the line of sight. Previous power spectrum analysis with CIBER data suggests that about half of the near-IR EBL photons originates from a diffuse component named "intra-halo light", the emission from stars tidally stripped from their parent galaxies which ultimately reside in the surrounding dark matter halo with an extended spatial distribution (Zemcov et al. 2014). In Cheng et al. 2020 in prep., we further constrain the IHL properties in CIBER imaging data using stacking analysis.

Optimal Mapping Strategy for Large-Scale Structure Survey

Cheng, Y.-T., de Putter, R., Chang, T.-C., & Dor√©, O. 2019, ApJ, 877, 86 (ADS, arXiv:1809.06384)

The conventional large-scale structure (LSS) surveys are based on galaxy detection (GD), whereas intensity mapping (IM) uses the integrated intensity to trace the underlying density field. In Cheng et al. 2019, we established a unified framework to describe both IM and GD using Fisher information. With this formalism, we have demonstrated that GD, IM, or their hybrid mode are optimal for different regimes of source confusion and the instrument noise level. We have also shown that this framework can serve as a tool to optimize the survey design and mapping strategy for future IM experiments.

Spectral Line De-blending in Line Intensity Mapping

Cheng, Y.-T., Chang, T.-C., Bock, J., Bradford, C. M., & Cooray, A. 2016, ApJ, 832, 165 (ADS, arXiv:1604.07833)
Cheng, Y.-T., Chang, T.-C., & Bock, J. 2020, ApJ, 901, xx (ADS, arXiv:2005.05341)

Line intensity mapping (LIM) (Kovtez et al. 2017 review paper) traces the three-dimension density field of the universe by mapping the emission from a certain individual spectral line, and infers the line of sight distance of the emission sources from the frequency-redshift relation. However, interloper foreground lines pose a serious challenge for LIM, where spectral lines from sources at different redshifts can end up at the same observed frequencies. For example, the [C II] intensity map from EoR will be mixed with several low-redshift CO rotational transition lines at the same observed frequency in LIM datasets. Masking and cross-correlation are the two most commonly-discussed line de-blending methods. However, they both rely on a high quality external dataset such as a deep galaxy catalog to effectively remove the interloper lines. During my PhD, I have developed two techniques for Fourier space and map space line de-blending that do not require the external information.

Fourier (Power Spectrum) Space Line De-blending
In LIM, the two-dimensional power spectrum of interloper lines will have anisotropic shape when projected onto a common coordinate frame. In Cheng et al. 2016, we presented a technique that can distinguish the large-scale fluctuation from different lines in LIM by this anisotropic feature in the power spectrum, and showed that this method can successfully extract both target and interloper line emission in a LIM survey targeting [C II] at EoR.

Phase-Space (Map-Space) Line De-blending
In Cheng et al. 2020, we develop a novel technique to perform line de-blending for LIM in the map space to reconstruct the three-dimensional spatial distribution of line-emitting sources. The method is based on the information that multiple spectral lines emitted by redshifted sources are mapped onto distinct observed frequencies, which give deterministic features in the observed spectrum that can be fitted by a template using a sparse approximation algorithm. We demonstrate a successful de-confusion for both TIME and SPHEREx survey setup using our method.

Research Highlights

Data-driven Cosmology from Three-dimensional Light Cones

Cheng, Y.-T., Wandelt, B., Chang, T.-C., & Doré, O. 2023, ApJ, 944, 151 (ADS, arXiv: 2210.10052)

To exploit the vast amount of information in large-scale surveys spanning multiple frequency bands, we have developed a novel data-driven technique for studying cosmology from a 3D light cone observed in multi-band large-scale images. Unlike traditional galaxy surveys that only detect bright sources or line intensity mapping, which only probes emissions from a specific spectral lines our approach harnesses information from "all photons". We simultaneously determine the spectral and spatial distribution of all emitting sources, as well as the underlying large-scale structure traced by them. Our method holds broad applicability for upcoming cosmological surveys and offers complementary information to detection-based galaxy redshift surveys such as SPHEREx, Roman, Euclid, etc.

EBL Tomography with SPHEREx-galaxy Cross Correlations

Cheng, Y.-T., Chang, T.-C., 2022, ApJ, 925, 136 (ADS, arXiv: 2109.10914)

The extragalactic background light (EBL) represents the cumulative emission from all extragalactic sources along the line of sight. The forthcoming NASA satellite mission, SPHEREx, aims to conduct an all-sky spectral mapping survey in the near-infrared wavelength range (0.75 - 5 um), offering an extraordinary dataset for investigating the EBL. In this study, we explore the potential of using galaxy cross correlations with SPHEREx data to constrain the spectral and redshift dependence of the EBL.

Probing the Intra-Halo Light in the Near-infrared Extragalactic Background Light

Cheng, Y.-T., Bock, J. 2022, ApJ, 940, 115 (ADS, arXiv: 2207.13712)
Cheng, Y.-T. + CIBER collaboration 2021, ApJ, 919, 69 (ADS, arXiv: 2103.03882)

Previous analyses of extragalactic background light (EBL) fluctuations have indicated that the intra-halo light (IHL) contributes significantly to the near-infrared background. The IHL refers to the emission originating from stars that have been tidally stripped from their parent galaxies and now reside in the surrounding dark matter halo, exhibiting an extended spatial distribution. In our study, Cheng et al. 2021, we employ a stacking analysis technique using CIBER imaging data to constrain the IHL around normal galaxies within the redshift range of 0.2 < z < 0.5. In Cheng & Bock 2022, we utilize simulations to characterize the dependence of the EBL power spectrum on nonlinear clustering scales arising from satellite galaxies and various IHL models.

Optimal Mapping Strategy for Large-Scale Structure Survey

Cheng, Y.-T., de Putter, R., Chang, T.-C., & Doré, O. 2019, ApJ, 877, 86 (ADS, arXiv: 1809.06384)

Conventional large-scale structure (LSS) surveys rely on galaxy detection (GD) methods, whereas intensity mapping (IM) utilizes the integrated intensity to trace the underlying density field. In Cheng et al. 2019, we developed a unified framework that describes both IM and GD using Fisher information. This formalism allows us to demonstrate that GD, IM, or a hybrid mode are optimal for different levels of source confusion and the instrument noise. Moreover, we have shown that this framework can be utilized as a valuable tool to optimize survey design and mapping strategies for future IM experiments.














Spectral Line De-blending in Line Intensity Mapping

Cheng, Y.-T., Chang, T.-C., Bock, J., Bradford, C. M., & Cooray, A. 2016, ApJ, 832, 165 (ADS, arXiv: 1604.07833)
Cheng, Y.-T., Chang, T.-C., & Bock, J. 2020, ApJ, 901, 142 (ADS, arXiv: 2005.05341)

Line intensity mapping (LIM) is a method used to trace the three-dimensional density field of the universe by mapping the emission from a specific spectral line. It allows for the inference of the line-of-sight distance of the emission sources based on the frequency-redshift relation. However, one of the major challenges for LIM is interloper foreground lines, where spectral lines from sources at different redshifts can overlap at the same observed frequencies. For instance,the [C II] intensity map targeting the Epoch of Reionization (EoR) can be mixed with several low-redshift CO rotational transition lines that share the same observed frequency. Commonly discussed line de-blending methods include masking and cross-correlation. These techniques rely on a high-quality external dataset, such as a deep galaxy catalog, to effectively remove the interloper lines. During my PhD, I have developed two techniques for line de-blending in Fourier space and map space, respectively. Notably, these methods do not require external information, offering an alternative approach to address the interloper foreground challenge in LIM analysis.

Fourier (Power Spectrum) Space Line De-blending
In the LIM, the two-dimensional power spectrum of interloper lines exhibits an anisotropic shape when projected onto a common coordinate frame. In Cheng et al. 2016, we introduced a technique that leverages this anisotropic feature in the power spectrum to distinguish the large-scale fluctuations arising from different lines in LIM. Furthermore, with simulated data, we demonstrated the successful application of this method in extracting both the target line emission and interloper line signals in a LIM survey targeting the [C II] line during the EoR.

Phase-Space (Map-Space) Line De-blending
In Cheng et al. 2020, we introduced a novel technique for performing line de-blending in LIM surveys. Our approach operates in map space and aims to reconstruct the three-dimensional spatial distribution of line-emitting sources. The method utilizes the fact that multiple spectral lines emitted by sources at different redshifts are mapped onto distinct observed frequencies, resulting in deterministic features in the observed spectrum. These features can be accurately modeled and fitted with templates based on a sparse approximation algorithm. We demonstrate a successful de-confusion for both TIME and SPHEREx survey setup using our method.

CIBER

CIBER (Cosmic Infrared Background ExpeRiment) was a sounding rocket experiment designed to probe the near-IR Extragalactic Background Light (EBL), the aggregate emission from all sources along the line of sight throughout cosmic time. CIBER conducted four flights between 2009 and 2013.

My main project with CIBER is to study the “intra-halo light”, the extended emission around galaxies, in near-IR EBL using the 4th flight CIBER imaging data (Cheng et al. 2021). I have taken the lead in developing an end-to-end data analysis pipeline for CIBER imagers.

Top: TIME grating spectrometer. Bottom: 2019 winter TIME engineering run at Kitt Peak.

TIME

TIME (Tomographic Ionized Carbon Intensity Mapping Experiment) is a mm-wavelength spectrometer designed for conducting [C II] line intensity mapping at redshifts 5 to 9. It will be deployed on an ALMA prototype antenna located at Kitt Peak, Arizona. By mapping the large-scale intensity field from the emission of the ionized carbon line [C II], TIME aims to probe the Epoch of Reionization (EoR) by characterizing the statistical properties of ionization sources. It will serve as a valuable complement to HI 21 cm intensity mapping and conventional galaxy detection methods.

My work on TIME involves simulating signals and foregrounds for the analysis pipelines, assisting with instrument deployment, and analyzing the instrument testing data. Additionally, I have developed two novel techniques to address the issue of interloper line foreground in intensity mapping. These techniques have the potential to be applied in future TIME data analysis.

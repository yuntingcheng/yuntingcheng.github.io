I am currently participating in two intensity mapping experiments: CIBER and TIME. I primarily work on theoretical modeling, pipeline development, and data analysis within these two collaborations.

CIBER

CIBER(Cosmic Infrared Background ExpeRiment) was a sounding rocket experiment designed to probe the near-IR Extragalactic Background Light (EBL), the aggregate emission from all sources along the line of sight throughout cosmic time. CIBER has flown four times between 2009 and 2013.

My research project with CIBER is to study the “intra-halo light”, the extended emission around galaxies, in near-IR EBL using the 4th flight CIBER imaging data (Cheng + CIBER collaboration 2020a in prep.). I have also led the end-to-end data analysis pipeline for CIBER imagers (Reseach).

Top: TIME grating spectrometer. Bottom: 2019 winter TIME engineering run at Kitt Peak.

TIME

TIME (Tomographic Ionized Carbon Intensity Mapping Experiment) is a mm-wavelength spectrometer designed to conduct [C II] line intensity mapping at redshift 5 to 9. TIME will be deployed on ALMA prototype antenna at Kitt Peak, Arizona. By mapping the large-scale intensity field from the emission of ionized carbon line [C II], TIME will probe the EoR by characterizing the statistical properties of the ionization sources, and will be complementary to the HI 21 cm intensity mapping and the conventional galaxy detection approaches.

My work on TIME includes simulating signals and foregrounds for the analysis pipelines, helping with instrument deployment, and analyzing the instrument testing data. In addition, I have also developed two novel techniques to tackle the interloper line foreground in intensity mapping that can be applied to TIME data analysis in the future (Reseach).
